I am a PhD student in astrophysics at the University of Utah working in X-ray astronomy and galaxy cluster physics under the advisement of Dr. Dan Wik. My research focuses on understanding the physical processes occurring in merging galaxy clusters, particularly the behavior of the intra-cluster medium during energetic merger events. I am especially interested in shock fronts, particle acceleration, plasma heating, and the interaction between thermal and nonthermal components of galaxy clusters.

My current work includes studies of the merging galaxy cluster Abell 754 using X-ray observations from NuSTAR and Chandra. One aspect of this research involves examining how electrons are heated across merger-driven shocks and comparing observational measurements to theoretical models of equilibration in the intra-cluster medium. I am also involved in collaborative work investigating radio relic systems and searching for Inverse Compton emission to better constrain magnetic field strengths in galaxy clusters.

I received my bachelor's degree in physics with an emphasis in astronomy from the University of Utah in 2021 and began graduate studies shortly afterward. Since joining the X-ray astronomy group, I have contributed to multiple research projects involving observational analysis, simulation work, and instrument studies. My first published project focused on simulated gas clumping in the outskirts of galaxy clusters and evaluating the sensitivity of the proposed STAR-X mission to these structures. My second publication focused on equilbration models in the merging cluster Abell 665.

In addition to research, I enjoy teaching and science communication. I mentor middle school students through Science with U, helping to develop and carry out science fair projects. I care deeply about making science approachable and engaging, and long term I hope to pursue a career as a professor where I can combine research, teaching, and mentorship.

Outside of astrophysics, I enjoy programming, Linux systems, visualization work, and developing tools that make scientific analysis more efficient and interactive. I am particularly interested in the intersection of computation, visualization, and astronomy, including ways of bringing astrophysical research into more accessible visual formats such as planetarium content and interactive media.
